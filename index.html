Symbolic Timeline
The Internet
1960s
ARPANET
Season 13
Origin of the internet traces back to the Advanced Research Projects Agency Network (ARPANET). It was the first wide-area packet-switched network with distributed control and one of the first computer networks to implement the TCP/IP protocol suite. Both technologies became the technical foundation of the Internet. ARPANET's main use was for academic and research purposes.
Development of TCP/IP
1970s
Development of TCP/IP.

TCP/IP was developed in the 1970s by computer scientists Robert Kahn and Vint Cerf. It was adopted as the protocol standard for ARPANET.

TCP/IP enable reliable end-to-end connections and data transfer between different systems. This laid the foundation for the internet and the worldwide web.

1971
Invention of email system
1971
In 1971, Ray Tomlinson, a computer engineer made history by inventing the email system as we know it today. Tomlinson’s groundbreaking work allowed for messages to be transmitted between computers connected to the ARPANET, a forerunner to the modern internet. By ingeniously utilizing the “@” symbol to distinguish the user’s name from the computer’s name, Tomlinson established a standardized addressing system for electronic messages. This game-changing innovation transformed communication by enabling individuals to send messages instantaneously across vast distances, laying the foundation for the interconnected world we inhabit today.
DNS (Domain Name System)
1985
DNS (Domain Name System)

In the early days of the internet, network hosts (computers and other devices that communicate with other computers and devices on the network) were identified and accessed using numerical IP addresses, for example, 192.0.2.1.

To deal with this problem a group including Jon Postel, Paul Mockapetris and Craig Partrige published RFC 882 which created the domain name system (DNS) to make Internet navigation easier.

DNS makes domain names like google.com or facebook.com instead of complicated strings of numerical characters.

1988
IRC (Internet Relay Chat)
IRC (Internet Relay Chat)

IRC was created by Jarkko Oikarinen in August 1988 to replace a program called MUT (MultiUser Talk) on a BBS called OuluBox at the University of Oulu in Finland.

The IRC is a real-time communication protocol and text-based chat system that allows people to send each other direct messages over the internet.

IRC has played a significant role in online communication and inspired many communication platforms in the market today.

The Beginning of the World Wide Web
1989
The Beginning of the World Wide Web

Sir Tim Berners-Lee, a British scientist, invented the World Wide Web (WWW) in 1989, while working at CERN.

Tim Berners-Lee wrote the first proposal for the World Wide Web in March 1989 and, in 1990, redistributed it.

His proposal outlined the key components and design principles of the World Wide Web:

  • The Uniform Resource Identifier (URI) provided a standardized way for identifying resources on the web.
  • The Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP) for transferring data over the internet.
  • The Hypertext Markup Language (HTML) to structure and format documents, which enabled the display of text, images, and links.
  • The notion of a distributed system for decentralized information to be accessible from any connected computer.
1990
Birth of Search Engines
Birth of Search Engines

The first internet search engines predate the debut of the Web in December 1990.

The first well documented search engine that searched content files, namely FTP files, was Archie, which debuted on 10 September 1990.

Archie could not search for content within web pages and would index only specific file formats. But it laid the groundwork for more advanced search engines of the 90s, like Gopher, Veronika, and Jughead, that began to index content on the web. They all led to landmark search engines, such as Web Crawler (1994), Lycos (1994), Alta Vista (1995), and eventually Google (1998)

The First Webpage
1991
The First Webpage

In 1991, Sir Tim Berners-Lee, the inventor of the World Wide Web, created yet another piece of pioneering technology - the first web page.

The first web page provided an overview of the WWW project, an explanation of hypertext, and instructions on how to create web pages. It also introduced hyperlinks that allowed users to navigate between web pages by clicking on text and images. The first web page’s address (or URL) was http://info.cern.ch/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html, and you can still access it today!

1993
Government Participation
Government Participation

In 1993, US President Bill Clinton introduced the National Information Infrastructure (NII), an initiative to develop policies and the necessary infrastructure to support the growth of the internet, ensure universal access, and address issues like privacy, security, and intellectual property rights.

The early 1990s marked a turning point when governments realized the internet was more than just technical infrastructure. They saw its far-reaching implications and began developing more comprehensive policies and frameworks for internet governance, regulation, and international cooperation. This shift was aimed at fostering the internet's continued growth and security as it was embraced globally.

First secure ecommerce transaction
1994
First secure ecommerce transaction

The very first secure online transaction went to Dan Kohn in August 1994. On August 11, Kohn sold a CD of Sting’s "Ten Summoner’s Tales" to a friend in Philadelphia and cost $12.48 plus $4.95 shipping.

This first online transaction utilized Netscape's Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) protocol to encrypt credit card data. SSL enabled secure transfers, providing the necessary trust for online payments.

1997
The Invention of Wi-Fi and WAP
The Invention of Wi-Fi and WAP

Wi-Fi is a wireless networking technology that allows devices such as computers (laptops and desktops), mobile devices (smart phones and wearables), and other equipment (printers and video cameras) to interface with the Internet. It allows these devices--and many more--to exchange information with one another, creating a network.

A wireless access point (AP) allows wireless devices to connect to the wireless network. Having a Cisco wireless network makes it easy to bring new devices online and provides flexible support to mobile workers.

Google
1998
Google

Google was founded on September 4, 1998, by American computer scientists Larry Page and Sergey Brin while they were PhD students at Stanford University in California.

More than 70 percent of worldwide online search requests are handled by Google, placing it at the heart of most Internet users’ experience. It is one of the world’s most prominent brands.

Google began as an online search firm, but it now offers more than 50 Internet services and products, from e-mail and online document creation to software for mobile phones and tablet computers.

Google’s broad product portfolio and size make it one of the top four influential companies in the high-tech marketplace, along with Apple, IBM, and Microsoft.

In 2015, Google was reorganized as a wholly owned subsidiary of Alphabet Inc. Google is Alphabet's largest subsidiary and is a holding company for Alphabet's internet properties and interests.

2000
The burst of the dot-com bubble
The burst of the dot-com bubble

The dotcom bubble was a rapid rise in U.S. technology stock equity valuations fueled by investments in Internet-based companies in the late 1990s.

The value of equity markets grew exponentially during the dotcom bubble, with the Nasdaq rising from under 1,000 to more than 5,000 between 1995 and 2000. Things started to change in 2000, and the bubble burst between 2001 and 2002 with equities entering a bear market.

The crash that followed saw the Nasdaq index, which rose five-fold between 1995 and 2000, tumble from a peak of 5,048.62 on March 10, 2000, to 1,139.90 on Oct. 4, 2002, a 76.81% fall. By the end of 2001, most dotcom stocks went bust. Even the share prices of blue-chip technology stocks like Cisco, Intel, and Oracle lost more than 80% of their value. It would take 15 years for the Nasdaq to regain its peak, which it did on April 24, 2015.

Social Media Networks
Early 2000s
Social Media Networks

In the early 2000s, several new social networking websites like Friendster, MySpace, and Facebook emerged, allowing people to connect and share information online in new ways.

These sites promoted online interactions through profiles, posts, media sharing, messaging, and real-time updates, forming virtual communities.

By mid to late 2000s, social media platforms like Facebook and Twitter attracted hundreds of millions of users globally, signaling a major cultural shift in how people interacted and communicated digitally.

2007
iPhone and Mobile web
TiPhone and Mobile web

For most people, the internet on mobile started with the rise of modern smartphones, specifically with the iPhone as the start of it.

The rapid advancement of technology in recent years has led to an increasing dependence on mobile devices for communication, entertainment, and access to information. The rise of the mobile internet has fundamentally transformed the way we live, work, and interact with each other.

2010 - Present
Present
2010 - Present

Social media platforms flourished and expanded their user base, smart technologies evolved, and e-commerce partly replaced regular shopping. This age saw the rise of influencer culture, streaming services, viral trends, mobile games, and the development of AI technologies, setting the trend for the future.

Pandemic

While our dependence on the internet was significant for both daily and professional purposes before the onset of Covid-19, it heightened even further during the pandemic. With Covid-19 emerging at the close of 2019, it prompted widespread lockdowns worldwide throughout 2020 and 2021. This, in turn, brought about a sustained era of social distancing, remote work, and a shift of our social interactions to online platforms.

Remote work and Learning

During the pandemic, people shifted to working, learning, communicating, and entertaining themselves from home through online tools. Platforms such as Zoom, Microsoft Teams, and Google Meet facilitated remote work, while educational institutions embraced e-learning platforms like Google Classroom or Microsoft Teams. Virtual classrooms and webinars became the norm. Simultaneously, with the rise in internet speed and widespread adoption of 5G, global connectivity is anticipated to expand, leading to the growth of the Internet of Things, connecting more devices and enabling smart vehicles, homes, cities, and industries. This surge in connectivity and smart device usage will necessitate enhanced security and privacy solutions.

Furthermore, the landscape of entertainment and work processes is expected to evolve with virtual reality and augmented reality technologies, transforming the way we work, consume content, and interact online. The ascent of artificial intelligence will also play a pivotal role in shaping the future of the internet.
